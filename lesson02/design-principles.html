Fitt's Law

Raising Cane's

RaisingCanes.com

Fitts’s law is a predictive model for the speed of human movement, commonly used in human-computer interaction. It states that the time it takes someone to select an object depends on how far they are from the object and the size of the object. Small objects that are far from your starting position or related objects that are far away from each other take the longest time to select. Large objects that are close to your starting position or related objects that are close together take the shortest time to select. (see blog.hubspot.com/marketing/fitts-law)
The website for Raising Cane's chicken finger restaurant illustrates this point by the location of the menu button as well as the button for ordering. The typical way a person handles their phone is with 2 hands gripping the device by the fingers as the thumbs are hovering over the screen. The location of the buttons makes the travel time of the thumbs very short. The buttons are also reasonable sized to proivide an obvious target for clicking. This design choice makes it easier and more likely for people to order from thier website.

Visual Hierarchy

Amazon

Amazon.com

Visual hierarchy is the principle of arranging elements to show their order of importance. Designers structure visual characteristics—e.g., menu icons—so users can understand information easily. By laying out elements logically and strategically, designers influence users’ perceptions and guide them to desired actions. (see interaction-design.org)
The website for Amazon is a good example of visual hierarchy. The search bar and menu are placed at the top of page for users to notice first. These tools are typically what the users are looking to use to find the item they came for. Below the top of the window, are the advertisments for sales and collections of items that might interest the user. These advertisments are larger and more visually interesting to draw the user to cliking those ads.

Hick's Law

Google

Google.com

Hick's Law states that the time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices presented. (see LawsofUX.com)
The main web page for Google is a prime example of Hick's law. The page is very simplistic and offers very few choices. The visual hierarchy draws the user to the first of very few choices, the search bar. Google.com wastes no time with other visual choices. Most users come to the website with the intent to use the search bar and the design of the page helps speed the user to their destination.
